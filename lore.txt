        <div class="elucidate-word-display">
          <div class="elucidate-word">Elucidate</div>
          <span class="elucidate-phonetic">/ ɪˈluːsɪdeɪt /  &nbsp;·&nbsp; verb &nbsp;·&nbsp; from Latin <em>elucidare</em> — to illuminate, to make clear</span>
        </div>

        <div class="elucidate-definition-block">
          <div class="def-panel literal">
            <div class="def-panel-label">Literal Meaning</div>
            <div class="def-panel-title">To Make Clear</div>
            <div class="def-panel-body">
              In its most direct sense, to <em>elucidate</em> is to shed light upon something that was previously obscured — to clarify, to explain, to bring into the open what was concealed by shadow or silence. It is an act of illumination applied not to physical space, but to <strong>understanding</strong>. A thing is elucidated when it can no longer be misread.
              <br><br>
              The Latin root, <em>elucidare</em>, joins <em>ex-</em> (thoroughly out) and <em>lucidus</em> (bright, clear). Note this carefully: the word <strong>Lucidus</strong> — the name given to Subject I-3 by the Church — is extracted from the same root. They named him after clarity. They gave him a name that means "clear" because they intended him to be uncomplicated. The cruelty is not accidental.
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="def-panel in-world">
            <div class="def-panel-label">Meaning Within the Game World</div>
            <div class="def-panel-title">To See What the World Hides</div>
            <div class="def-panel-body">
              Within the world of the DLC, to elucidate is the central act of every playable character's story. Each character begins in a state of <em>enforced obscurity</em> — they have been deliberately prevented from understanding who they are, where they come from, and why the world is in the condition it is in. The game is about the process of elucidation: the painful, costly, irrevocable act of learning the truth.
              <br><br>
              <strong>Ignis cannot elucidate without losing the identity built on falsehood.</strong> Funeris cannot elucidate without confronting what she was created to be. Father Calum cannot elucidate without admitting what he has spent thirty years refusing to feel. The word is both the goal of every protagonist and the source of every devastation in the story.
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="def-panel symbolic">
            <div class="def-panel-label">Symbolic Interpretation</div>
            <div class="def-panel-title">The Cost of Light</div>
            <div class="def-panel-body">
              Symbolically, the act of elucidation in this world carries <em>price</em>. Light is not free. Every revelation in the game's narrative costs something: a relationship, an identity, a certainty, a peace of mind once held. The Church promises clarity through Lumen, but its clarity is the clarity of control — knowing what you are permitted to know and calling that knowledge sufficient.
              <br><br>
              True elucidation — the kind that the game's characters pursue across three paths each — is <strong>not the same as peace</strong>. It is the opposite. It destabilizes. It makes the Mind State climb. It forces choices that cannot be unmade. The game asserts something radical: that <em>understanding the truth is more dangerous than ignorance</em>, and that it is nonetheless the only thing worth pursuing.
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="def-panel connection">
            <div class="def-panel-label">Connection to Core Themes</div>
            <div class="def-panel-title">Knowledge · Truth · Perception · Reality</div>
            <div class="def-panel-body">
              <strong>Knowledge:</strong> In this world, knowledge is weaponized. The Church controls information. The Cult guards it through ritual. The Merchant collects it at personal cost. To know something is to become dangerous — and dangerous people are either eliminated or used.
              <br><br>
              <strong>Truth:</strong> Truth is distinguished from fact throughout the narrative. The Church presents facts: dates, names, mission parameters. Truth is what lies beneath the facts — the reason the orphanage existed, the reason a child was called a designation rather than a name.
              <br><br>
              <strong>Perception &amp; Reality:</strong> The Mind State system literalizes this theme. As perception degrades, <em>reality itself changes for the character</em>. Eldritch Horrors look different at different Mind State thresholds. The game treats the question of what is real as a variable — something that shifts based on who is looking and in what condition they are looking.
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>

        <div class="elucidate-philosophical">
          <p>
            There is a question that runs underneath every conversation in this game, never asked directly, never answered cleanly: <strong>is it better to know?</strong> The Church would say no — that clarity is given, not found, and that the institution's job is to protect its people from the unbearable clarity of certain truths. Father Calum has organized his entire life around this principle. He believed, for thirty years, that what he was doing was an act of mercy.
          </p>
          <p>
            The Merchant would say yes — that not knowing is its own form of corruption, and that the silence of witnesses is the foundational material from which institutions of harm are built. He walked past that orphanage fence at sixteen years old and understood something terrible, and then he kept walking, and he has been trying to walk back ever since. The cost of his eventual courage is a measure of how much his earlier silence cost him.
          </p>
          <p>
            <em>Elucidate</em> — the word, the act, the theme — sits at the exact point where these two positions meet and refuse to resolve. The game does not conclude that knowing is good. It concludes that <strong>not knowing is not neutral</strong>. Ignorance, in this world, is always manufactured. The choice is not between knowing and not knowing. The choice is between pursuing elucidation at cost, or accepting the comfortable arrangement someone else has built around your permanent blindness. The orphanage children could not have known what they were walking into. But the adults who watched them go — and said nothing — made a choice. Every silence in this world is a choice. Every elucidation is a rebellion.
          </p>
        </div>
      </div>
    </section>

    <section class="terminology-section" id="terminology-anchor">
      <div class="terminology-container">
        <div class="section-header">
          <span class="section-tag">Official Glossary · Terms &amp; Definitions</span>
          <h2 class="section-title">Terminology <span class="gold">Archive</span></h2>
          <p class="section-desc">Each term in this archive is both a mechanical concept and a narrative one. Understanding how they connect is understanding how the world works.</p>
        </div>

        <div class="terms-filter-bar">
          <button class="term-filter-btn active" data-filter="all">All Terms</button>
          <button class="term-filter-btn" data-filter="cat-mind">Mind &amp; Psyche</button>
          <button class="term-filter-btn" data-filter="cat-caligo">Caligo &amp; Dark</button>
          <button class="term-filter-btn" data-filter="cat-lumen">Lumen &amp; Order</button>
          <button class="term-filter-btn" data-filter="cat-world">World &amp; Place</button>
          <button class="term-filter-btn" data-filter="cat-horror">Entities &amp; Horrors</button>
        </div>

        <div class="terms-grid" id="terms-grid">

          <!-- MIND STATE -->
          <div class="term-card cat-mind reveal" data-searchable="Mind State Corruption psychological stability meter threshold">
            <span class="term-tag">Mind &amp; Psyche</span>
            <div class="term-name">Mind State</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              The primary mechanical and narrative system measuring a character's <strong>psychological stability</strong>. Represented as a value from 0 to 100, where 0 is complete stability and 100 is total identity collapse. Mind State rises through exposure to Caligo influence, combat trauma, revelation shock, and environmental corruption. It falls through rest, Blue Gem consumption, and specific companion interactions.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              The Mind State system is the game's central argument about the relationship between knowledge and psychological cost. Every act of elucidation — every truth revealed — raises Mind State. The system does not punish knowledge as wrong; it acknowledges that knowledge has weight, and that weight accumulates. Characters with higher MS resistance (Father Calum, 5/5) have spent years or decades building suppression mechanisms. Characters with lower resistance (Funeris, 1/5; Pluvia, 1/5) feel the weight of the world's truth immediately and without buffer.
            </div>
            <div class="term-example">
              At MS 100, identity collapses. Caligo's ambient influence fills the void left by a shattered sense of self. Not a dramatic monster death — a quiet disappearance into darkness.
            </div>
          </div>

          <!-- CORRUPTION -->
          <div class="term-card cat-caligo reveal" data-searchable="Corruption Caligo ambient influence deterioration zones">
            <span class="term-tag">Caligo &amp; Dark</span>
            <div class="term-name">Corruption</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              The ambient deterioration of physical reality, biological structure, and psychological coherence caused by proximity to <strong>Caligo's influence</strong>. Corruption is not a supernatural attack — it is the natural consequence of exposure to something whose operational scale exceeds what physical and mental structures were built to accommodate. It does not intend harm; it simply is, and contact with it distorts what it touches.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              Both the Church and the Cult have been shaped by Corruption in ways they fail to recognize. The Church's institutional violence is a form of social corruption — the deterioration of the principle it claims to represent. The Cult's obsessive rituals around Caligo exposure are a different form of the same deterioration. Corruption in this world is not limited to the Zones of Terrors. It is also present in every institution that claims to manage it while secretly replicating its effects in different language.
            </div>
            <div class="term-example">
              The most subtle corruption in the game is administrative language: "let them rest" for "acknowledge the fatality." The words rot before the bodies do.
            </div>
          </div>

          <!-- ENLIGHTENMENT -->
          <div class="term-card cat-lumen reveal" data-searchable="Enlightenment Lumen clarity truth understanding revelation">
            <span class="term-tag">Lumen &amp; Order</span>
            <div class="term-name">Enlightenment</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              In Church doctrine, Enlightenment is the state of complete alignment with Lumen's principles: clarity of purpose, suppression of doubt, unwavering service to the institution's order. In practice, this is a state the Church manufactures through conditioning, identity suppression, and the careful management of information access. True Enlightenment — in the philosophical sense the word suggests — would be incompatible with the Church's continued operation, as it would require seeing the institution clearly.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              The game reframes Enlightenment as an act of rebellion rather than compliance. Every character who achieves true self-knowledge does so against the Church's explicit design. Ignis's Path B ending — in which he destroys the institution that built him and reclaims the name they buried — is the game's closest approximation of what genuine Enlightenment would look like: not peace, but clarity; not comfort, but truth.
            </div>
          </div>

          <!-- FRAGMENTS -->
          <div class="term-card cat-caligo reveal" data-searchable="Fragments Caligo physical manifestation three shards power">
            <span class="term-tag">Caligo &amp; Dark</span>
            <div class="term-name">Fragments of Caligo</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              Physical manifestations of Caligo's influence made tangible — pieces of something that was never meant to be fragmentable. Three exist in the game world. The first is held by the Tribe and passed to whoever rescues the Shaman. The second is in the Merchant Guild's possession in Port City. The largest is guarded at the heart of Cultist Island by the Cult Leader himself. All five playable characters are, in one way or another, entangled in their fate.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              The Fragments function as the game's MacGuffin and its central thematic object simultaneously. The Church wants them for control. The Cult treats them as sacred. Neither perspective is wrong about their power; both are catastrophically wrong about what that power should be used for. A recovered document in the Theocratic Capital archive reveals that Church containment protocols and Cult ritual use of the Fragments "share more methodology than either party would admit."
            </div>
            <div class="term-example">
              Pluvia gives the Fragment to the Mercenary with the words: "I give it to you because my Foresight tells me that you will use it better than either of them, and worse than you should, and it will cost you something. That is still the best outcome I can see."
            </div>
          </div>

          <!-- NEXUS POINTS -->
          <div class="term-card cat-world reveal" data-searchable="Nexus Points Zones geography high concentration Caligo">
            <span class="term-tag">World &amp; Place</span>
            <div class="term-name">Nexus Points</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              Specific geographic locations within the Zones of Terrors where Caligo's influence is most concentrated — places where the ambient pressure of proximity to the Formless has accumulated to such a degree that the boundary between ordinary reality and Caligo's influence becomes functionally permeable. At Nexus Points, <strong>Caligo Manifestations</strong> are present, Mind State rises at accelerated rates (+15), and the physical environment distorts in ways that go beyond standard Zone corruption.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              Funeris's encounter with a Caligo Manifestation at a Nexus Point produces one of the game's most philosophically precise observations: the Manifestation is not hostile, not conscious, not responsive. It simply expands and contracts. The cultists who survive the Zones of Terrors are the ones who understand this — not the ones who pray to it, but the ones who learned to move around it without disturbing it. Faith directed at something indifferent is not answered. But indifferent things can still be navigated.
            </div>
          </div>

          <!-- ZONES OF TERROR -->
          <div class="term-card cat-world reveal" data-searchable="Zones of Terrors high risk areas Caligo corruption uninhabitable">
            <span class="term-tag">World &amp; Place</span>
            <div class="term-name">Zones of Terrors</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              Areas of the world where Caligo's ambient corruption has reached a density sufficient to make normal habitation impossible and sustained exposure psychologically lethal. Defined by accelerated Mind State increase rates (+3/min passive), unpredictable Eldritch Horror spawning, and the visual distortion of environment and entity appearance based on the character's current MS. The Church built its experiment program specifically to create soldiers who could operate in these zones without losing coherence.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              The Zones are the world's most honest spaces. They do not pretend. The corruption is visible, measurable, honest in its danger. By contrast, the Theocratic Capital is a Safe Zone in which far more damage is done in far more concealed ways. The game's use of Zone classification — Safe, High-Risk, Dual-State — is a structural irony: the safe places are often where the most harm is administered. The dangerous places are simply dangerous in ways you can see.
            </div>
          </div>

          <!-- LUMEN ENERGY -->
          <div class="term-card cat-lumen reveal" data-searchable="Lumen Energy order light clarity Church principle cosmic">
            <span class="term-tag">Lumen &amp; Order</span>
            <div class="term-name">Lumen Energy</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              The ambient presence of Lumen's cosmic principle in the world: the force of order, structure, and clarity that defines edges, names things, makes language and recognition possible. In its pure form, Lumen Energy is genuinely stabilizing — it anchors Mind State, clarifies perception, and reinforces a character's sense of self and purpose. In Church-mediated form, it is carefully dosed: enough to maintain loyalty and coherence, not enough to enable independent evaluation.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              The Chapel of Lumen encounter in the Walled City illustrates the difference precisely. At low Mind State, interacting with the altar provides stable, comforting lore about the Lumen faith. At high Mind State, the text begins to distort: words repeat, sentences reverse, the Mercenary's hands shake on the page. Lumen Energy is not corrupted by the Mind State — but the ability to receive it clearly is. The Church uses this to its advantage: soldiers in controlled psychological states absorb Lumen doctrine without friction. The experiment program is, in part, a system for manufacturing and maintaining that controlled state permanently.
            </div>
          </div>

          <!-- CALIGO INFLUENCE -->
          <div class="term-card cat-caligo reveal" data-searchable="Caligo Influence ambient corruption Mind State horrors formless">
            <span class="term-tag">Caligo &amp; Dark</span>
            <div class="term-name">Caligo Influence</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              The ambient permeation of Caligo's presence into physical space, biological matter, and psychological architecture. Unlike Lumen Energy, Caligo Influence does not require proximity to a formal source — it exists as background radiation in any space where the balance between order and chaos has been significantly disrupted. It is most concentrated in the Zones of Terrors and at Nexus Points, but traces are measurable everywhere, including in the Theocratic Capital's Underground Laboratory. The experiment program was built to make subjects resistant to it. Some subjects became more <em>sensitive</em> instead — and were classified as failures.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              Funeris's "defect" — her heightened Caligo sensitivity — reframes the entire moral architecture of the experiment program. The Church discarded her because she could feel too much. The Cult recruited her because she could feel too much. Neither institution understood that her sensitivity was not a malfunction in the design; it was the design's most complete outcome. A person who can feel the wrongness of the world before they can name it is not broken. They are paying attention.
            </div>
            <div class="term-example">
              "The Church built labs and procedures to make soldiers who could survive this. And they threw me away for being too sensitive to it." — Funeris, entering a Zone of Terrors
            </div>
          </div>

          <!-- ELDRITCH HORROR -->
          <div class="term-card cat-horror reveal" data-searchable="Eldritch Horror entity Mind State appearance changes humanoid">
            <span class="term-tag">Entities &amp; Horrors</span>
            <div class="term-name">Eldritch Horrors</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              Entities that manifest in regions of high Caligo Influence, particularly in the Zones of Terrors and the Home Village. Humanoid in general shape, they are <strong>wrong in proportion</strong> — the wrongness itself varying in degree with the observer's Mind State. At low MS, they appear as something between a man and a shadow. At mid-range MS, proportions shift: too many joints, too few edges. At high MS, they become something else entirely — massive, amalgamated, moving in ways that feel like a violation of what movement is supposed to be.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              The Eldritch Horrors are the game's most explicit statement about the Mind State system: what you see depends on who you are when you're looking. They do not change. The player's perception of them does. This mechanic teaches, through gameplay, that the horror of the world is not a fixed external quantity — it is proportional to how much of your own stability you have retained. The experiment program's purpose was to create people who could see these entities at full clarity without breaking. Most of the orphanage children could not do this. Subject I-3 could. That is, in a precise technical sense, what the Church means when it describes him as their "finest instrument."
            </div>
          </div>

          <!-- FORESIGHT -->
          <div class="term-card cat-mind reveal" data-searchable="Foresight Pluvia Shaman ability future vision visions MS cost">
            <span class="term-tag">Mind &amp; Psyche</span>
            <div class="term-name">Foresight</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              Pluvia's unique ability to perceive possible futures — short windows of time ranging from seconds (tactical) to weeks (strategic) ahead of the present moment. Active Foresight is player-triggered and reveals enemy positions, traps, and hidden information at a cost of MS +10 per use. Passive Foresight triggers automatically at story events, showing short clips of possible futures. Both become less reliable and more psychologically costly as Mind State rises, eventually becoming indistinguishable from hallucination.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              Foresight is described by Pluvia as leaning into a river current rather than against it. The metaphor is precise: the current is always moving, always pushing. Using Foresight means moving with forces that exist regardless of whether you engage them. The cost is that the more you lean into the current, the more you forget which way the flow runs — the more the future begins to replace the present as your primary experience of reality. Pluvia's Path C ending, in which she can no longer locate the boundary between what is and what might be, is the logical conclusion of a gift with no sustainable off switch.
            </div>
          </div>

          <!-- ORPHANAGE -->
          <div class="term-card cat-world reveal" data-searchable="Orphanage origin all characters unifying thread experiments children">
            <span class="term-tag">World &amp; Place</span>
            <div class="term-name">The Orphanage</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              The single location that connects all five playable characters before any of them are consciously aware of the connection. A Church-run institution that served as the primary supply point for the underground experiment program: children with no family, no one to ask questions, no one to come looking. The Church selected specifically for this absence of connection — they chose children nobody would miss, and then they ensured that any connection the children formed with each other was systematically removed before deployment.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              The Orphanage is referenced in dialogue before it is visited. The note in the Walled City armory — "Ask about the orphanage" — is the game's invitation to pay attention to a thread it has been laying since the first scene. When the Mercenary finally walks through the ruins of the Home Village and hears the memory fragments playing from the well and the dormitory walls, the horror is not that the place was terrible. It is that it was mundane. Administrative. Clean on the surface. The horror the Church is best at is the kind that looks, from the outside, exactly like an ordinary charitable institution doing ordinary charitable work.
            </div>
          </div>

          <!-- MIND STATE RESISTANCE -->
          <div class="term-card cat-mind reveal" data-searchable="MS Resistance Mind State baseline character suppression capacity">
            <span class="term-tag">Mind &amp; Psyche</span>
            <div class="term-name">Mind State Resistance</div>
            <div class="term-definition">
              Each playable character has an inherent MS Resistance rating from 1/5 to 5/5, representing their baseline capacity to absorb psychological pressure without deterioration. This is not courage or intelligence — it is <strong>suppression architecture</strong>: the set of mechanisms, habits, conditioning, and self-deception strategies each character has built over time. Father Calum (5/5) has the highest resistance not because he is the strongest character morally, but because he has had the most practice not feeling things he should feel. Funeris (1/5) has the lowest because she was expelled before that architecture could be fully built.
            </div>
            <div class="term-context-label">Narrative Context</div>
            <div class="term-context">
              The resistance ratings invert conventional heroism. The character with the most psychological fortitude by the game's mechanical standards is also the character who has done the most harm over the longest period of time. Funeris's vulnerability is inseparable from her sensitivity — the same quality that makes her break faster also makes her feel more accurately. The game suggests, quietly, that high MS resistance is not an asset to be aspired to. It is a description of damage sustained and buried, not damage avoided.
            </div>
          </div>

    <section class="origin-section" id="origin-anchor">
      <div class="origin-container">
        <div class="section-header">
          <span class="section-tag">Historical Record · Chronological Flow</span>
          <h2 class="section-title">World <span class="gold">Origin</span> &amp; <span class="caligo-c">History</span></h2>
          <p class="section-desc">From cosmic equilibrium to a world consumed by institutional war — the sequence of events that brought everything to the state it is in when the player arrives.</p>
        </div>

        <div class="origin-flow">

          <div class="origin-step reveal">
            <div class="origin-spine"><div class="origin-dot gold-d"></div><div class="origin-line"></div></div>
            <div class="origin-content">
              <div class="origin-era">Before Recorded History</div>
              <div class="origin-step-title">Cosmic Equilibrium — Lumen and Caligo in Balance</div>
              <div class="origin-step-body">
                In the beginning, the world existed in fragile balance between <em>Lumen</em> and <em class="caligo-em">Caligo</em> — order and chaos, light and formless darkness. Neither was wholly good nor evil. They were two poles of existence. When balance held, the world prospered. When it was broken, the world decayed. No human institution existed yet to worship or weaponize either principle. They simply were. This is the state the game implies has been irrecoverable since the Church's founding.
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>

          <div class="origin-step reveal">
            <div class="origin-spine"><div class="origin-dot gold-d"></div><div class="origin-line"></div></div>
            <div class="origin-content">
              <div class="origin-era">Ancient Era</div>
              <div class="origin-step-title">The Church of Lumen Rises</div>
              <div class="origin-step-body">
                Human ambition found the principle of Lumen and built an institution around it. The Church began as an honest attempt to organize human society around order and clarity. Over generations, institutional growth, political ambition, and the accumulated small compromises of men and women who told themselves their decisions were for the greater good transformed it into something that uses the language of illumination to justify the systematic suppression of truth. <em>The doctrine is not Lumen. The doctrine is a perversion of it.</em> Lumen itself remains as real and present as it ever was. The Church simply holds a monopoly on who is permitted to access it, and at what cost.
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>

          <div class="origin-step reveal">
            <div class="origin-spine"><div class="origin-dot caligo-d"></div><div class="origin-line"></div></div>
            <div class="origin-content">
              <div class="origin-era">Period of Expansion</div>
              <div class="origin-step-title">Caligo's Ambient Corruption Spreads</div>
              <div class="origin-step-body">

const inputEl   = document.getElementById('search-input');
const overlayEl = document.getElementById('search-results-overlay');

function performSearch() {
  const q = inputEl.value.trim().toLowerCase();
  overlayEl.innerHTML = '';
  if (!q || q.length < 2) { overlayEl.classList.remove('visible'); return; }

  const results = loreSearchIndex.filter(item =>
    item.title.toLowerCase().includes(q) ||
    item.excerpt.toLowerCase().includes(q) ||
    item.section.toLowerCase().includes(q)
  ).slice(0, 10);

  if (!results.length) {
    overlayEl.innerHTML = '<div class="search-no-result">No results found for "' + escapeHtml(q) + '"</div>';
  } else {
    results.forEach(item => {
      const div = document.createElement('div');
      div.className = 'search-result-item';
      div.innerHTML = `<div class="sr-title">${highlightMatch(item.title, q)} <span style="font-size:9px;color:var(--text-dim);margin-left:6px;">${item.section}</span></div><div class="sr-excerpt">${highlightMatch(item.excerpt.slice(0,90) + '…', q)}</div>`;
      div.onclick = () => {
        const target = document.getElementById(item.id);
        if (target) target.scrollIntoView({ behavior: 'smooth', block: 'start' });
        overlayEl.classList.remove('visible');
        inputEl.value = '';
      };
      overlayEl.appendChild(div);
    });
  }
  overlayEl.classList.add('visible');
}

function highlightMatch(text, q) {
  const idx = text.toLowerCase().indexOf(q);
  if (idx < 0) return escapeHtml(text);
  return escapeHtml(text.slice(0, idx)) +
    '<mark class="search-highlight">' + escapeHtml(text.slice(idx, idx + q.length)) + '</mark>' +
    escapeHtml(text.slice(idx + q.length));
}
function escapeHtml(str) {
  return str.replace(/&/g,'&amp;').replace(/</g,'&lt;').replace(/>/g,'&gt;').replace(/"/g,'&quot;');
}

inputEl.addEventListener('input', performSearch);
inputEl.addEventListener('keydown', e => {
  if (e.key === 'Escape') { overlayEl.classList.remove('visible'); inputEl.blur(); }
});
document.addEventListener('click', e => {
  if (!e.target.closest('.search-wrap') && !e.target.closest('#search-results-overlay')) {
    overlayEl.classList.remove('visible');
  }
});

const revealObserver = new IntersectionObserver((entries) => {
  entries.forEach(entry => {
    if (entry.isIntersecting) {
      entry.target.style.opacity = '1';
      entry.target.style.transform = 'translateY(0)';
    }
  });
}, { threshold: 0.06, rootMargin: '0px 0px -30px 0px' });
