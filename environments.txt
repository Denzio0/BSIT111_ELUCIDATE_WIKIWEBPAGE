      <div class="section-header">
        <span class="section-tag">Cosmology</span>
        <h2 class="section-title">The <span class="gold">World of Elucidate</span></h2>
        <p class="section-desc">Before light gave the world its name, there was only the formless dark.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="lore-intro-block">

        <div class="lore-chapter reveal">
          <div class="lore-chapter-label">Chapter I — Origin</div>
          <h3 class="lore-chapter-title">Before the First Light: The Age of Caligo</h3>
          <div class="lore-body">
            <p>The world did not begin with creation. It began with <span class="lore-caligo">Caligo</span> — an absence so total, so vast in its formlessness, that to look upon it directly was to feel your own edges dissolve. The ancient texts do not call it evil. They call it <em>precedent</em>. Caligo predated everything: the mountains, the seas, the living things that would one day name themselves and fight wars over those names. It had no will. No preference. It existed the way weather exists — without intention, without malice, and without mercy.</p>
            <p>For ages beyond reckoning, the world breathed in this darkness. Strange organisms adapted to it. Philosophies were built within it. And those who survived long enough in the black developed a relationship with Caligo that was less devotion and more <span class="lore-emphasis">mutual accommodation</span> — you do not worship the tide that swallows you; you simply learn which shores to avoid.</p>
          </div>
          <div class="lore-pull-quote">"The darkness does not hate the candle. It simply persists after the candle goes out." — Recovered inscription, Theocratic Capital Library</div>
          <div class="lore-body">
            <p>Then came light. Not the slow warming of a dawn, but a rupture — a <span class="lore-lumen">Vindication Lumen</span>, a cosmic declaration that illumination was not merely possible but <em>correct</em>. The event fractured the world's relationship with itself. Where Caligo had been the universal medium of existence, it was now a <span class="lore-caligo">contaminant</span>, a thing to be purged, an enemy defined by its opposition to something new.</p>
          </div>
        </div>

        <div class="lore-chapter reveal">
          <div class="lore-chapter-label">Chapter II — The Conflict</div>
          <h3 class="lore-chapter-title">Vindication Lumen and the Church's First Lie</h3>
          <div class="lore-body">
            <p>The Church of Lumen was not built on falsehood. In its earliest form it was a genuine order — scholars and practitioners who had witnessed the Vindication and sought to understand what it meant to live in a world where light and darkness were now in active opposition. They built temples not to control but to <span class="lore-lumen">comprehend</span>. They named the cosmic force Lumen and offered themselves as its intermediaries.</p>
            <p>But comprehension is expensive to maintain. Control is simpler to sell. Over generations, the scholars became administrators. The administrators became generals. The temples became fortresses. By the time the Church declared its first Holy War against the Cult of Caligo, it had so thoroughly inverted its own principles that its holy texts had been quietly rewritten at least four times — each revision granting the ruling body slightly more authority, slightly more justification, slightly more reason why the suffering of others was cosmically sanctioned.</p>
            <p>The Cult of Caligo, by contrast, was never a philosophy. It was <span class="lore-caligo">a consequence</span>. Failed Church experiments. Orphans discarded when they proved insufficient. Soldiers abandoned in zones of eldritch corruption. People who had looked into the dark for so long that the dark had looked back — and offered something the Church never would: acknowledgment of what they truly were.</p>
          </div>
        </div>

        <div class="lore-chapter reveal">
          <div class="lore-chapter-label">Chapter III — The Corruption</div>
          <h3 class="lore-chapter-title">Subject I-3 and the Spread of the Fracture</h3>
          <div class="lore-body">
            <p>The Church's most catastrophic error was not its wars or its doctrine or even its underground laboratories where children were remade into weapons. Its most catastrophic error was <span class="lore-caligo">Subject I-3</span> — the experiment that survived when it should not have, that thrived when it should not have, and that carried within it a piece of Caligo's ancient presence like a wound that never stopped weeping.</p>
            <p>When Subject I-3 escaped containment, it did not flee. It <em>walked</em>. Through the laboratory corridors, through the restricted wings, through the capital, and out into the world. And wherever it moved, the geography of the land shifted slightly — not visibly, not measurably, but in the way a room shifts when someone leaves it who was not supposed to be there at all. Trees leaned at wrong angles. Water ran toward high ground. The sky remembered Caligo and turned colors it had not been permitted to turn since before the first Lumen burned.</p>
            <p>These became the <span class="lore-caligo">Zones of Terrors</span>. Not places Caligo chose, but places that chose themselves — points where the membrane between the world's true nature and its imposed order grew thin enough to see through. The Church declared them blasphemous. The Cult called them sacred. The people who actually lived near them simply moved away and tried not to look back.</p>
          </div>
          <div class="lore-pull-quote">"Every region you will walk through has been shaped by something it cannot explain and cannot forget. That is the nature of this world. That is the nature of you." — Pluvia (Shaman), to the Mercenary</div>
        </div>

      </div>

const inp = document.getElementById('search-input');
const ovr = document.getElementById('search-results-overlay');

function doSearch() {
  const q = inp.value.trim().toLowerCase();
  ovr.innerHTML = '';
  if (!q || q.length < 2) { ovr.classList.remove('visible'); return; }
  const results = searchData.filter(i =>
    i.title.toLowerCase().includes(q) ||
    i.excerpt.toLowerCase().includes(q) ||
    i.section.toLowerCase().includes(q)
  ).slice(0, 10);
  if (!results.length) {
    ovr.innerHTML = '<div class="search-no-result">No results found.</div>';
  } else {
    results.forEach(item => {
      const d = document.createElement('div');
      d.className = 'search-result-item';
      d.innerHTML = `<div class="sr-title">${hl(item.title, q)} <span style="font-size:9px;color:var(--text-dim);margin-left:6px;">${item.section}</span></div><div class="sr-excerpt">${hl(item.excerpt.slice(0,80)+'…', q)}</div>`;
      d.onclick = () => {
        const target = document.querySelector(item.anchor);
        if (target) { target.scrollIntoView({ behavior: 'smooth', block: 'start' }); }
        ovr.classList.remove('visible');
      };
      ovr.appendChild(d);
    });
  }
  ovr.classList.add('visible');
}

function hl(text, q) {
  const i = text.toLowerCase().indexOf(q);
  if (i < 0) return esc(text);
  return esc(text.slice(0,i)) + '<mark class="search-highlight">' + esc(text.slice(i,i+q.length)) + '</mark>' + esc(text.slice(i+q.length));
}
function esc(s) { return s.replace(/&/g,'&amp;').replace(/</g,'&lt;').replace(/>/g,'&gt;').replace(/"/g,'&quot;'); }

inp.addEventListener('input', doSearch);
inp.addEventListener('keydown', e => { if (e.key === 'Escape') { ovr.classList.remove('visible'); inp.blur(); } });
document.addEventListener('click', e => {
  if (!e.target.closest('.search-wrap') && !e.target.closest('#search-results-overlay')) ovr.classList.remove('visible');
});
