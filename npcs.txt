              <p>Blacksmith Conrad has equipped soldiers for the Church of Lumen long enough to know which ones come back. He has been watching them for years — watching the ones who ask questions and the ones who don't, cataloging the difference in how they walk out of the workshop, noting which set of eyes belongs to a man who is thinking and which belongs to a man who has stopped. The Blunderbuss he gives Ignis will, in Path B, be used to help end the Church that commissioned its creation. Conrad might not find that ironic. He might find it inevitable.</p>
              <p><strong>Relationship to Ignis:</strong> Conrad tells Ignis to keep asking questions. He does this without preamble or explanation. For a man who equips soldiers without names for wars without endings, this is an unusual act of investment. The game implies that Conrad has seen what happens when the questions stop. He is trying, in the only way available to him, to make sure this one doesn't.</p>

              <p>The Draft Officer is the Church of Lumen as it presents itself to the world: efficient, impersonal, entirely comfortable with the opacity of its own processes. He knows no more than he needs to know and appears to have made peace with this long ago. When Ignis asks what the Fragments are, the Draft Officer's response is not evasion — it is honest institutional description. The Church operates on a need-to-know basis at every level, and the Draft Officer genuinely does not know what he has not been told.</p>
              <p>This makes him, paradoxically, one of the game's more honest characters. He does not pretend the briefing sheets are useful. He does not dress up his ignorance in authority. He simply delivers the mission with the practiced efficiency of someone who has been delivering missions for a long time and has learned that soldiers who ask too many questions before deployment tend to receive less satisfying answers than soldiers who simply go.</p>
              <p><strong>Connection to the World:</strong> The Draft Officer represents the Church's outer face — the layer of institutional structure that processes people without understanding what is being processed. He is not complicit in the experimental program in any way he would recognize. He receives orders. He issues orders. In the Theocratic Capital's architecture of controlled information, he is one of many people whose function depends on not knowing what their function serves. This is, the game implies, the Church's most reliable defense: the sincere ignorance of its own administrators.</p>
              <p><strong>Significance:</strong> His early line — "You'll learn what they want you to know when they want you to know it" — lands differently on replay. It is not a warning he intends to give. But it is, structurally, the first warning Ignis receives about the nature of the institution he serves.</p>

              <p>The Librarian Scholar is the most dangerous person in the Theocratic Capital, and he is dangerous specifically because no one in authority has noticed. He maintains the Cathedral Library with impeccable institutional loyalty — the shelves are organized, the records are current, the approved summaries are available in their approved forms. And then, when a soldier with one eye asks for the real history of Caligo, he gives it. Quietly. Precisely. And then he smiles, tucks his book back onto the shelf, and walks away as if he has said nothing remarkable at all.</p>

He contextualizes the Church's founding not as a response to evil but as a response to influence — and then poses the question that the entire narrative is built around. Is the Church trying to protect the world from Caligo, or trying to be the only entity allowed to use it? This question, posed in the first hour of gameplay, is answered definitively only in the final acts.</p>
              <p><strong>Significance to the World:</strong> The Librarian Scholar represents what the game identifies as the rarest institutional type: a person who works within a corrupt system and uses that position to incrementally undermine it, without spectacle, without martyrdom, and without any certainty that any individual conversation will matter. He is playing a very long game with very small moves. Ignis is one of them.</p>

              <p>The confession booths along the Cathedral's east wall are among the game's most quietly devastating design choices. Each booth contains a civilian NPC who will whisper their testimony to anyone who enters — stories of missing children, soldiers returned wrong, experiments that left no visible mark except in the eyes. They speak in the dark because the light belongs to the Church, and the Church's light does not illuminate what happened to their children.</p>
              <p>None of these witnesses are combatants. None of them have special abilities or mission-critical information. They have what the game's other information sources cannot provide: the ground-level human cost, described by the people who absorbed it without being able to name it. A nephew who came back calling his aunt 'civilian.' Soldiers who could not meet your eyes anymore. Children who were special and then were not the same.</p>
              <p><strong>Connection to Ignis and the Orphanage:</strong> At MS above 50, Ignis begins to recognize fragments of the booth testimonies as his own. Not explicitly — not as a cutscene revelation — but in the way a specific phrase or cadence will surface a memory that was not supposed to be there. This is the game's most precise use of environmental storytelling: the booth witnesses are giving Ignis pieces of his own history from the outside, in the voices of the people who watched the carriages and could not stop them.</p>
              <p><strong>World Impact:</strong> The booth witnesses collectively represent the Church's civilian footprint — the ordinary families who have been quietly absorbing the cost of the Vindication Lumen program for decades without a framework to understand what they experienced. They are not organized. They are not resistant. They are simply people with grief they cannot explain, whispering it into the dark in the one space the Church designed for private testimony.</p>

Twenty years of operating a harbor-front tavern have given her an intelligence network assembled not through any deliberate design but through the simple, persistent reality that people talk more freely in taverns than in offices, and she has been listening. He is not a man who dramatizes danger. He states it, professionally, with the flat affect of someone who has watched what happens to people who underestimate a crossing and has lost the desire to soften that information for anyone's comfort. The Harbor Captain is a hinge point — the last neutral figure any character will encounter before the world becomes definitively dangerous.</p>

              <p>The Guild Master represents the game's most honest expression of institutional cowardice — and the game handles him with notable fairness. He is not a villain. He is a man who knows what Konrad knows, has known it longer, and has spent longer finding ways to continue functioning within a system he recognizes as corrupt. His argument against Konrad's investigation is not self-interested in any simple way: he is genuinely warning Konrad that the Church controls half the Guild's contracts and that exposure will burn the Guild alongside it.</p>

They have watched the same carriages. The Guild Master resolved it by becoming very good at not watching. Konrad resolved it by watching until he could not justify the watching anymore. The conversation between them is not an argument. It is two resolutions to the same impossible situation, standing in the same room, each recognizing the cost of the other's choice.</p>
              <p><strong>World Impact:</strong> The Guild Master's silence — maintained for decades — represents the Church's most effective tool: the cooperation of people who know, who could act, and who have calculated that they cannot afford to. In a world where Caligo's corruption spreads through broken balance and institutional decay, the Guild Master is the institutional decay. Not through malice. Through exactly the kind of reasonable, considered, long-term self-preservation that keeps systems alive long past their moral expiration.</p>

// ─── NPC EXPAND TOGGLE ───────────────
function toggleNPC(btn) {
  const body = btn.closest('.npc-body');
  const lorePanel = body.querySelector('.npc-lore-full');
  const isOpen = lorePanel.classList.contains('open');
  lorePanel.classList.toggle('open', !isOpen);
  btn.classList.toggle('open', !isOpen);
  btn.childNodes[0].nodeValue = isOpen ? 'Full Lore ' : 'Hide Lore ';
}

// ─── SCROLL REVEAL ───────────────────
const observer = new IntersectionObserver((entries) => {
  entries.forEach(entry => {
    if (entry.isIntersecting) {
      entry.target.classList.add('revealed');
      observer.unobserve(entry.target);
    }
  });

function escapeHtml(str) {
  return str.replace(/&/g,'&amp;').replace(/</g,'&lt;').replace(/>/g,'&gt;').replace(/"/g,'&quot;');
}
function highlightMatch(text, q) {
  const idx = text.toLowerCase().indexOf(q);
  if (idx < 0) return escapeHtml(text);
  return escapeHtml(text.slice(0,idx)) + '<mark class="search-highlight">' + escapeHtml(text.slice(idx, idx+q.length)) + '</mark>' + escapeHtml(text.slice(idx+q.length));
}

function performSearch() {
  const q = inputEl.value.trim().toLowerCase();
  overlayEl.innerHTML = '';

  // Inline card filter
  let visibleCount = 0;
  document.querySelectorAll('.npc-card').forEach(card => {
    const searchStr = (card.dataset.search || '') + ' ' + (card.querySelector('.npc-name')?.textContent || '');
    const activeFilter = document.querySelector('.filter-btn.active')?.dataset.filter || 'all';
    const zone = card.dataset.zone || '';
    const zoneMatch = activeFilter === 'all' || zone === activeFilter;
    const textMatch = !q || q.length < 2 || searchStr.toLowerCase().includes(q);

    if (zoneMatch && textMatch) {
      card.style.display = '';
      visibleCount++;
    } else {
      card.style.display = 'none';
    }
  });

  document.getElementById('no-results').classList.toggle('visible', visibleCount === 0 && q.length >= 2);

  if (!q || q.length < 2) { overlayEl.classList.remove('visible'); return; }

  const results = searchData.filter(item =>
    item.title.toLowerCase().includes(q) || item.excerpt.toLowerCase().includes(q)
  ).slice(0, 7);

  if (!results.length) {
    overlayEl.innerHTML = '<div class="search-no-result">No results for "' + escapeHtml(q) + '"</div>';
  } else {
    results.forEach(item => {
      const div = document.createElement('div');
      div.className = 'search-result-item';
      div.innerHTML = `<div class="sr-title">${highlightMatch(item.title, q)} <span style="font-size:9px;color:var(--text-dim);margin-left:6px;">${item.section}</span></div><div class="sr-excerpt">${highlightMatch(item.excerpt.slice(0,80)+'…', q)}</div>`;
      div.onclick = () => { overlayEl.classList.remove('visible'); };
      overlayEl.appendChild(div);
    });
  }
  overlayEl.classList.add('visible');
}

inputEl.addEventListener('input', performSearch);
inputEl.addEventListener('keydown', e => { if (e.key === 'Escape') { overlayEl.classList.remove('visible'); inputEl.blur(); } });
document.addEventListener('click', e => {
  if (!e.target.closest('.search-wrap') && !e.target.closest('#search-results-overlay')) {
    overlayEl.classList.remove('visible');
  }
});
